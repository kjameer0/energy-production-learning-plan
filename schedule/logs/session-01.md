## What's still murky

**Gap 1 — Proton gradient (Exercise 3):**
Answered "assembly line organization" but missed the key mechanism. The inner mitochondrial membrane creates a sealed compartment that allows a proton gradient to build up. ATP synthase is driven by protons flowing back across that gradient — it's not just organization, it's the physical basis of energy capture. Open cytoplasm would let protons diffuse freely and destroy the gradient.

**Gap 2 — "Borrowed energy" at the cellular level (Exercise 4):**
Interpreted the article's framing through cognitive load / neural drive rather than cellular mechanism. The more grounded reading: stress hormones mobilize stored reserves (glycogen, fat) — energy set aside for later gets pulled into circulation now. That's the borrowing. The instinct that total ATP production might be unchanged is actually the correct critical point — but it needs to be grounded in the reserve-mobilization mechanism.

**Gap 3 — Cyanide transfer (Metacog):**
Said "ATP synthase can't receive oxygen." More precisely: cyanide blocks Complex IV of the electron transport chain. This stops proton pumping, the gradient collapses, ATP synthase stops because there's nothing driving it, and NADH/FADH₂ back up with nowhere to go. ATP synthase is downstream of the blockage, not the site of it.
