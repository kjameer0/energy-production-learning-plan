## What this node is

The schema-building peak of this module. Allostatic load is the concept that unifies everything before it: it is the cumulative physiological cost of chronic stress across all the systems cortisol touches. Understanding it gives you a framework that applies to diet, relationships, sleep, work, and exercise simultaneously — which is why it is the right lens for evaluating the article's claims.

## Sources

- **Primary (short, foundational):** McEwen & Stellar (1993), "Stress and the Individual: Mechanisms Leading to Disease." *Archives of Internal Medicine*, 153(18), 2093–2101.
  Search PubMed (pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov) for: McEwen Stellar 1993 allostatic load. This is the paper that named the concept. ~8 pages, readable without a clinical background.

- **Secondary (broader context):** McEwen (1998), "Protective and Damaging Effects of Stress Mediators." *New England Journal of Medicine*, 338(3), 171–179.
  More accessible than the 1993 paper for a non-clinician. Available via PubMed.

- **Optional:** Sapolsky, *Why Zebras Don't Get Ulcers*, Chapter 14 — "Stress and Depression." Sapolsky uses the accumulated stress framework extensively here, connecting it to mood and cognitive function.

## Schema-building exercises

1. **Define it precisely.** Allostatic load is not just "a lot of stress." Write a definition in your own words that captures: (a) what allostasis is, (b) what the "load" refers to, and (c) why it accumulates rather than resetting cleanly.

2. **The four pathways.** McEwen describes four ways allostatic load builds up: frequent stress, failure to habituate, failure to shut off the response, and inadequate response forcing other systems to compensate. Describe a concrete life situation that illustrates each pathway. These don't have to be extreme — everyday examples are more instructive.

3. **The ledger.** Allostatic load is sometimes described as a ledger with withdrawals and deposits. What counts as a withdrawal? What counts as a deposit? Where does sleep fit? Exercise? A draining relationship? Use the framework to evaluate at least one thing in your own life.

4. **Back to the article.** The article says "your body directs energy toward whatever it's organized around." Is that a reasonable summary of allostatic load theory, or is it imprecise? What does it get right, and what does it miss?
