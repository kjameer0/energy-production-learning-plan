## What this node is

A fluency-light, schema-focused introduction to what ATP is, why cells need it, and what "cellular energy production" actually means biologically. This is the foundation that makes phrases like "mitochondrial function," "oxidative metabolism," and "energy production" meaningful rather than rhetorical.

## Sources

## Fluency exercises

Fluency-light node — schema only. See exercises below.

## Schema-building exercises

1. **Define it without jargon.** Explain what ATP is and what it does in a single sentence, as if to someone with no biology background. Then explain why a cell that runs out of ATP dies, using the same constraint.
	1. 

2. **Trace the chain.** Starting from "you eat a meal," trace the steps by which the energy in food ends up as ATP available to a muscle cell. You don't need to name every intermediate — just the major stages and where they happen in the cell.

3. **Why mitochondria?** The textbook describes mitochondria as the site of most ATP production. Why does this happen in a specialized organelle rather than in the cell's general interior? What does the membrane structure give you that open cytoplasm doesn't?

4. **Connect to the module.** The article we started with claims that "stress chemistry" produces "borrowed energy." Now that you know what ATP is, what would it mean for energy to be "borrowed"? What would actually have to happen at the cellular level for that framing to be accurate?

## Metacog checks

Run these before advancing to node 02.

- **Explanation**: Can you explain what ATP is, what mitochondria do, and why oxygen matters for energy production — without looking at the source?
- **Transfer**: A person is poisoned with cyanide, which blocks the final step of the electron transport chain. What happens to ATP production, and therefore to the cell? (You don't need to know what cyanide is — reason from the mechanism.)
- **Connection**: How does this node connect to the claim in the article that "energy is what separates the living from the dead"? Is that claim accurate given what you now know, or is it imprecise?
