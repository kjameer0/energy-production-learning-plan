## Schema-building exercises

These are the applied transfer exercises. Work through them in order — each one is harder than the last.

1. **Claim triage.** Read the article's section headers and classify each major claim into one of three buckets:
   - *Well-supported by the stress-energy model* (your nodes 01–05 provide a mechanistic basis)
   - *Plausible but needs more evidence* (the mechanism is coherent but the article doesn't establish it)
   - *Not supported or distorted* (the claim doesn't follow from the mechanism, or the mechanism it assumes is wrong)
   Do this for at least six claims.

2. **The thyroid section.** The article devotes significant space to thyroid function and makes specific causal claims (cortisol → rT3, estrogen → adaptive energy depletion, T3 conversion in the liver). For each claim: (a) is the mechanism real, (b) does the article's framing accurately represent it, and (c) what would a reader need to look up to verify it independently? Be specific.

3. **The Ray Peat question.** The article is heavily influenced by Ray Peat, a biologist who operated outside peer review. Without knowing anything about Peat specifically, what should you look for when evaluating claims from an author in that position? Write a short checklist (4–6 items) that you would apply to any health writer who cites unusual sources or operates outside mainstream medicine.

4. **Your own life.** Using the allostatic load framework from node 04, identify one thing in your own life that is likely a meaningful contributor to allostatic load. Be specific about the mechanism — not just "work is stressful" but which cortisol pathway it activates and what downstream system it probably affects most. This is the transfer test for the whole module.
