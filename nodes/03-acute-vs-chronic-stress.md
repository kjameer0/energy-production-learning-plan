## What this node is

The distinction between stress as an adaptive short-term response and stress as a chronic state that damages the systems it was designed to protect. This is the node where the biology of stress becomes directly relevant to everyday life — and where most popular health writing goes wrong by conflating the two.

## Sources

- **Primary:** Sapolsky, *Why Zebras Don't Get Ulcers* (3rd ed.)
  - **Chapter 1** (revisit the second half) — the setup about why a sustained stress response becomes harmful.
  - **Chapter 4** — "Stress, Metabolism, and Liquidating Your Assets." Directly addresses how the stress response redirects energy in the short term and what happens when that redirection becomes permanent.
  Estimated time: 40–50 minutes.

## Schema-building exercises

1. **The adaptive case.** Describe the acute stress response from the perspective of a zebra being chased by a lion. Walk through what happens physiologically in the first 30 seconds, the next 5 minutes, and after the threat resolves. Why is each phase adaptive?

2. **Where it breaks down.** The same system that saves the zebra is harmful to a human sitting in traffic. Identify at least three specific ways the acute stress response becomes destructive when it is never fully turned off. Be mechanistic — name what is being suppressed or elevated and why that causes harm over time.

3. **The energy redirection problem.** Chapter 4 uses the phrase "liquidating assets." What does Sapolsky mean? What systems does the body deprioritize during a stress response, and what does it prioritize? Why is this a reasonable trade-off for a 3-minute threat and a bad one for a 3-year career crisis?

4. **Apply to the article.** The article we started with describes two energy states. Using what you now know about acute vs. chronic stress, evaluate the article's claim that stress-hormone-driven energy is "borrowed." What is accurate about that framing, and what does it leave out or distort?

## Metacog checks

Run these before advancing to node 04.

- **Explanation**: Can you explain the difference between acute and chronic stress in terms of the underlying physiology — not just "one is short and one is long"?
- **Transfer**: A person works a high-pressure job for five years, then quits and takes a calm job. Would you expect their stress physiology to normalize immediately? Why or why not?
- **Connection**: How does this node connect to nodes 01 and 02? Specifically: what happens to ATP production during an acute stress response, and what happens to it during chronic stress?
